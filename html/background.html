Basic Principles of Water and Soil

Soil is made up of lots of different particles with pores between them. When water is added it fills the pores between the particles, which saturates the soil. Eventually, the gravitational water flows downwards, leaving air gaps and only the capillary and hygroscopic water in the pores. The space between particles act as capillary tubes, holding the capillary water in place using the attraction and hydrogen bonds between the soil and water. As the water content in the soil reduces, the radius of the menius reduces and the capillary water is held in greater tension.

  • Saturated - pores are completely full of water
  • Unsaturated - pores contain both water and air

Plants require an air supply at their roots, so need to be surrounded by unsaturated soil. Unsaturated soil can exist at many different moisture content levels, but plants require the moisture to be between wilting and field capacity:

  • Wilting Capacity - the moisture content where plants can no longer take up any of the water
  • Field Capacity - the moisture content of the soil once gravitational water has flown away

The Wetting Front

After irrigation water infiltrates in an outward sphere through the surrounding soil, creating a moving boundary between wet and dry soil. This boundary is the wetting front. It moves downwards due to the motion lowering the gravitational potential energy of the water and it moves horizontally due to capillary action by the surrounding dry soil; water always flows to a lower pressure head. So the position of the wetting front depends on:

  • Amount of water added
  • Soil Composition (grain size/pore size)
  • Factors removing water (plant roots and evaporation)

Why do we care about the position of the wetting front?

Ideally, farmers want the wetting front to cover roughly 2/3 of the active root zone, as this ensure that the plants can take up the optimum amount of water needed. If the wetting front is:
  • Too Shallow - the roots won't be properly covered so the crops won't receive enough water
  • Too Deep - when the wetting front goes beyond the active root zone water is being wasted and the water is likely to wash away the key soil nutrients, which reduces the crop yield

So by allowing farmers to locate the depth of the wetting front they are able to irrigate appropriately, which enables optimum water usage and maximises crop yield.

Ideal Wetting Front Depth

The ideal depth depends on the root distribution of each individual crop. Roots aren't distributed uniformly downwards; the majority of the root mass is accumulated much higher than the maximum root depth. This means that to cover 2/3 of the active root zone the wetting front doesn't need to go down anywhere near the maximum root depth.
