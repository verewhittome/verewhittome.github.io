The Problem Statement

Optimise water usage in Ethiopian agriculture

An Engineering Approach

  • Aid Ethiopian farmers in detecting the wetting front so that farmers can carry out irrigation optimally.
  • Follow the UNICEF principles of digital design
  • Keep costs lower than $30 to remain affordable for Ethiopian farmers (current solution “fullstop” is at least $60)

Basic Principles of Water and Soil

Soil is made up of lots of different particles with pores between them. When water is added it fills the pores between the particles, which saturates the soil. Eventually, the gravitational water flows downwards, leaving air gaps and only the capillary and hygroscopic water in the pores. The space between particles act as capillary tubes, holding the capillary water in place using the attraction and hydrogen bonds between the soil and water. As the water content in the soil reduces, the radius of the menius reduces and the capillary water is held in greater tension.

  • Saturated - pores are completely full of water
  • Unsaturated - pores contain both water and air


Plants require an air supply at their roots, so need to be surrounded by unsaturated soil. Unsaturated soil can exist at many different moisture content levels, but plants require the moisture to be between wilting and field capacity:

  • Wilting Capacity - the moisture content where plants can no longer take up any of the water
  • Field Capacity - the moisture content of the soil once gravitational water has flown away


The Wetting Front

After irrigation water infiltrates in an outward sphere through the surrounding soil, creating a moving boundary between wet and dry soil. This boundary is the wetting front. It moves downwards due to the motion lowering the gravitational potential energy of the water and it moves horizontally due to capillary action by the surrounding dry soil; water always flows to a lower pressure head. So the position of the wetting front depends on:

  • Amount of water added
  • Soil Composition (grain size/pore size)
  • Factors removing water (plant roots and evaporation)


Locating the Wetting Front - The Solution

Ideally, farmers want the wetting front to cover roughly 2/3 of the active root zone, as this ensure that the plants can take up the optimum amount of water needed. If the wetting front is:

  • Too Shallow - the roots won't be properly covered so the crops won't receive enough water
  • Too Deep - when the wetting front goes beyond the active root zone water is being wasted and the water is likely to wash away the key soil nutrients, which reduces the crop yield

So by allowing farmers to locate the depth of the wetting front they are able to irrigate appropriately, which enables optimum water usage and maximises crop yield.


Ideal Wetting Front Depth

The ideal depth depends on the root distribution of each individual crop. Roots aren't distributed uniformly downwards; the majority of the root mass is accumulated much higher than the maximum root depth. This means that to cover 2/3 of the active root zone the wetting front doesn't need to go down anywhere near the maximum root depth. The most common cash crops grown in Ethiopia and their ideal wetting front have been found:

Crop Depth of 50% Root Mass /cm Depth of 95% Root Mass /cm Ideal Wetting Front Depth /cm
Wheat 16.8 103.8 25
Barley 11.5 99.6 22
Chickpea 17.4 84.8 30
Pulse Crops 15.3 83.6 28

Each concept will be evaluated by each of these key design metrics, with the weightings indicating the importance of the criteria (on a scale of 1 to 5 with increasing importance). The performance each concept will be compare to the FullStop detector's performance, giving a comparative score between +2 and -2. +2 = much better than the datum, +1 = better than the datum, 0 = equal to the datum, -1 = worse than the datum, -2 = much worse than the datum. Then the comparison score is scaled by it's metric's weighting and each value is summed up to give a quantitative evaluation of each concept.


Each design metric will be assessed in the following way:

  • Accuracy - how far the wetting front moves beyond the device before detection
  • Price - material cost and estimated labour cost
  • Locally Produced - percentage of the raw materials and product manufacturing done in Ethiopia
  • Durability - assessed on flammability, fresh water resistance, salt water resistance, UV (sunlight resistance) and wear resistance. These are all taken from the CUED materials data book
  • Time per Measurement - amount of time spent using the device (assembly/installation and time spent taking each measurement/resetting)
  • Environmentally Friendly
    • - qualitative investigation into manufacture of the raw materials, their environment impact during use (i.e. do they leach into the surrounding soil) and their end of life/reuse
