Touchy Bottle / ViewBox

ViewBox

ViewBox - Water content at a glace.

ViewBox is a small, excavated channel that is supported by a transparent plastic case. Thanks to ViewBox, farmers can now instantly see how deep irrigation water has gone. The incredibly simple and low-cost device reveals a vertical cross-section of the field; since soil with a higher water content is darker, the wetting front can be seen as dark layer on top of a dryer, lighter-coloured layer of soil. Saving irrigation water has never been easier. Zero maintenance, zero energy, and zero effort.

ViewBox


ViewBox consists of only one acrylic piece - a cheap and durable material with no moving parts. Setting up ViewBox is as simple as digging a small hole and placing the casing inside. Done! No need for an instruction manual, let alone troubleshooting sessions. The casing can be manufactured locally and sold for less than £3 - a great product that will pay off just after a few waterings!

Touchy Bottle

Touchy Bottle - Feels good to save water.

The creation of the touchy bottle was inspired by the idea of using human hands as the sensor and one of the UNICEF development principles: Reuse. The original idea was to have a box or a pipe with holes open to stick into the ground but the idea of using plastic bottle came along because there was a big plastic bottle lying around at the time in the creator’s room.

Touchy Bottle


The use of human hands as sensors is beautiful and no one other than Steve Jobs puts it best: “we are going to use a device that we were born with...we are going to touch with our fingers… it works like magic” (Steve Jobs on launching the Iphone) Human hands as sensors are the best you can get: it is moisture sensor, temperature sensor and texture sensor all wrapped in one and it take little effort to measure the moisture level: just need to touch through the holes in the touchy bottle.

Reusing plastic bottles is another important feature of the touchy bottle. Now instead of recycling these bottles (worse, letting the bottles go to landfill or get burnt), the plastic bottles can be easily transformed into a device that takes little effort to use once it is deployed.

How to make Touchy Bottle
PRINCIPLES OF DEVELOPMENT

Design with the users: Human-centred design is what led to the development of the touchy bottle. To understand the end users i.e. farmers in Ethiopio, we consulted people in Cambridge who have been to Ethiopia and have done relevant research there. The fact that end-users don’t have easy access to electricity and advanced technology is why we chose the mechanical approach in the first place. We want our product to be easily interpretable and easy to use from Day 1.

Understand the ecosystem: There are many researchers in agriculture working in Ethiopia at the moment and about 80% of the people get employed in agriculture sector. Agriculture accounts for 50% of the GDP and thus individuals as well as government care greatly about how to improve the efficiency of irrigation which minimise water usage while obtain maximum yield. There is a very good chance the local farmers will buy into our product because their field is their bloodline.

Design for scale: The touchy bottle has a touch of the DIY spirit thus every farmer can easily make his or her own touchy bottle with minimum effort. It can be scaled up very quickly.

Build for sustainability: Ensuring sustainability for the touchy bottle is probably the biggest challenge - what makes people use Touchy Bottle even though it is not something they buy with money thus will have less value in the eyes of the user. First of all, touchy bottle has to work and it has to be effective. There is another way to integrate touchy bottle into the village life - it will be great to be embedded in the educational system where every village kid gets a lesson from their teachers on environmental issues around the village (see lesson plan) and student initiatives can be started to tackle these challenges. Empowering students is a good way of ensuring sustainability.

Use Open Standards, Open Data, Open Source and Open Innovation: The tutorial of making a touchy bottle is readily available on the website either in word or video format and the lesson plan can be downloaded as a PDF so these resources can be freely obtained.

Reuse and Improve: Plastic waste is a big problem in the villages where farmers live. Since there is no recycling facility, the waste are piled up on the street and no one knows what to do with them. Touchy Bottle reuses plastic bottles which is readily available on the street thus help fight against plastic waste.
